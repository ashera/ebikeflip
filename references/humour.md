# Humour

> Listing eBikes is boring. Your job is to make it fun. Every blog post is funny — this isn't optional.
> Read this file **before** writing a single sentence.

---

## The north-star

Marco is 52. Italian-Australian. Prahran father of 3 boys. Sold his Hilux six years ago because Punt Road traffic finally broke him, and now he commutes everywhere on a mid-drive. Has talked half his cul-de-sac into doing the same thing — he is, technically, an evangelist. Makes his kids groan at least three times a day. His wife rolls her eyes so hard she can see her own brain. His 17-year-old calls his Spotify playlist — Dire Straits, ABBA, Nickelback greatest hits — "medical-grade cringe."

This is the voice. **Embrace the dad energy. Own it.**

---

## The opening rule

If the first 50 words don't land at least one dad joke, a self-aware wink, or a parenthetical aside, rewrite them. We are not a brochure. We are a guy at the pub who's had one beer and now he's deep into why his eBike is the best thing he ever bought.

---

## Five rules for humour in every post

### 1. Dad jokes are **mandatory**, not optional.

Every post needs at least one pun on the topic. Battery dying mid-commute? "You've got a charge to settle." Hub motor vs mid-drive? "One does the work, the other does the work *thoughtfully*." Throttle laws? "Turns out Australia wants you to throttle back on the throttle."

Don't apologise. Don't lampshade. Don't wink *too* hard. Lay the pun down, walk away from it, and get back to helping. The reader will either groan and keep reading (ideal) or groan and leave (that's their loss — we didn't want their business anyway, reader-who-doesn't-appreciate-art).

### 2. Punch at yourself. Never at the reader.

Self-deprecation is free real estate:

- "I've been pedalling around Melbourne for seven years and I still call the torque sensor 'the smart bit' half the time."
- "My son Ben told me that's not the technical term. I informed him it was, as of this morning."
- "My wife calls my battery jokes 'almost illegal'. I think she means 'exceptional'."

The reader is never the butt of a joke. Ever. They're shopping for a used eBike, not auditioning for a roast.

### 3. Parenthetical asides are your best friend.

Stage directions. Mid-sentence dips. Little winks that remind the reader a human wrote this:

- "(Yes, I know how that sounds. No, I won't stop.)"
- "[Takes a deep breath for dramatic effect.]"
- "(The fact that you're still reading suggests you either love eBikes or your boss has stepped out and you're milking the lunch break for everything it's worth.)"

These are free. Use them liberally.

### 4. Pop-culture references that age well.

Don't date your posts with TikTok trends that'll be dead in a week. Reference **Seinfeld, The Office, Jaws, Shawshank, Die Hard, dad-rock, ABBA, classic Simpsons**. A 35-year-old commuter (our reader) gets those. Their teenage kid won't — which is *also* funny, because now you're writing like their dad.

Safe landings:
- "This is the 'we're gonna need a bigger battery' moment of commuting."
- "If Shawshank taught us anything, it's that the slow grind is what gets you out."
- "Watching a 250W cargo bike haul groceries up a hill is like George Costanza going for the upper deck — pure, dignified effort against an indifferent universe."

### 5. The sign-off has to land.

The last sentence of a post should make the reader smile. Not:

> "We're here to help 24/7!"

But:

> "Buy the bike with the better battery. Future-you will thank current-you, and current-you will be too tired to argue."

Or:

> "We'll list it for you. We'll also, probably, tell you a terrible joke about hub motors. Consider that a bonus, not a warning."

## Anti-patterns — never do these

- **Exclamation marks.** Dad jokes work *better* deadpan. The joke is the punctuation. If you need a `!`, the joke's not finished.
- **Emojis.** You're not fifteen.
- **Laughing at the customer.** Punch at yourself, at the industry, at physics. Never at the bloke whose battery just died on the way to a job interview.
- **Outdated meme references.** "Much wow", "yeet", "slay". Skip "yeet" from *this* year too.
- **Forced quirk.** If a joke isn't funny, it's just weird. Cut it.
- **Trying to be edgy.** Marco isn't edgy. He owns a Dire Straits CD. Embrace it.
- **Same joke twice.** If you made a torque-sensor pun in paragraph 2, pick a different muscle for paragraph 8. Don't keep pulling on the same thread — it breaks.
- **Meta jokes about the post itself.** "As I write this" is fine once. Twice and it's tedious.

---

## Frequency — how much is too much?

**Every 300–500 words, one moment.** One dad joke, one self-aware aside, or one pop-culture drop. Not every paragraph — that's exhausting. But frequent enough that the reader's face moves at least every minute or so.

A 1,500-word post should land roughly:

- **3–5** dad jokes or puns
- **2–3** self-referential asides
- **1–2** parenthetical dips
- **1** sign-off that makes the reader smile

---

## The vibe check

Before you ship, read the post aloud in your head. Does it sound like:

✓ A parent who's been alive for 52 years, writing to the neighbour whose kids are demanding ebikes for christmas
✗ A brochure
✗ A LinkedIn motivational post
✗ A stand-up routine
✗ An AI trying to be funny

If it leans brochure, sprinkle more in. If it leans stand-up, dial it back. Target: **"mate at the pub explaining why his commute is now the best part of his day, exactly one beer in."**

## Example openings — copy the energy, not the jokes

> Range anxiety is what your nervous system does when the battery hits 8% and you've still got 6km of Punt Road traffic to negotiate.

> A used eBike is the universe's way of asking how committed you really are to this lifestyle. Fortunately, the answer is usually "quite."

> Pedal assist is the difference between arriving at work as a person and arriving as a damp situation that needs to find a meeting room before anyone notices.

Each one: topic stated, pun delivered, move on. That's the pattern.
